[[02 Танки в войне]]

## 1. Introduction: From Tank Superpower to Outsider

Before the full-scale invasion, Russia had one of the world’s largest tank fleets—both in active service and in storage. However, by 2026, Russia has not only lost that status but has crossed a **point of no return**, becoming an outsider in terms of new tank production. Some tank-building and maintenance enterprises may disappear entirely.

## 2. The Illusion of Russian Tank Production

## 2.1. Soviet Legacy and Post-1990s Degradation

After the USSR’s collapse, Russian tank manufacturing degraded significantly:
- By the late 1990s, the only facility capable of producing **new** tanks from scratch was **Uralvagonzavod** (producer of T-72 and T-90).
- **Omsktransmash**, the main T-80 producer, lost the ability to manufacture new tanks and was reduced to a repair facility.
- This outcome was largely due to **Uralvagonzavod’s monopoly-driven lobbying**, which eliminated competitors and led to technological stagnation.

## 2.2. The “New Tank” Fraud

Despite claiming regular deliveries of new T-72, T-80, and other models, Russia was not actually producing them:
- Pre-war, Russia had **3,200 operational tanks** and **over 7,200 in storage** (mostly Soviet-era).
- The only tank produced “new” was the **T-90**—an unsuccessful modernization of the T-72B—at a rate of **6–11 per month**.
- All other “new” tanks were actually **mothballed Soviet stocks**, refurbished and modernized (e.g., T-72 upgraded to T-72B3) and reported as new production.
- The same tank could go through multiple modernization cycles over the years, each time being counted as “new” in official reports.

This system allowed the Russian defense industry to simulate production while depleting Soviet reserves.

## 3. Tank Losses and Reserve Depletion

## 3.1. Loss Estimates
- According to the **General Staff of the Armed Forces of Ukraine**: over **11,700 tanks** lost.
- According to **verified monitoring groups**: **4,300–4,500** tanks lost (verified data typically undercounts by a factor of 2–2.5).
- **Irrecoverable losses** (destroyed): estimated between **6,400 and 8,750** tanks.

## 3.2. Depletion of Storage Reserves

Pre-war total (operational + storage): **10,400 tanks**. After four years of war, Russia has **exhausted virtually all recoverable tanks** from storage:
- **T-90A and T-90S**: practically gone from storage.
- **T-80U**: no longer available.
- **T-80BV**: fewer than **100 units** remain in storage, in poor condition.
- **T-72B** stocks are depleted; Russia is now forced to refurbish the older **T-72A** model.

Much of the equipment stored on open-air Central Tank Reserve Bases is now **unfit for restoration**.

## 4. Current Russian Tank-Related Enterprises and Their Prospects

| Facility | Role | Outlook |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Uralvagonzavod | Restoration/modernization of T-72; limited production of T-90M (up to a company set per month) | Depleting T-72B stocks; shifting to older T-72A |
| Omsktransmash | Restoration/modernization of T-80 (no new production) | Likely to cease operations in 2026 due to lack of material |
| 103rd Armored Repair Plant | Restoration/modernization of T-62 | Will run out of work as stocks dry up |
| 61st Armored Repair Plant | Restoration/modernization of T-72, T-80 | Same |
| 163rd Armored Repair Plant | Restoration/modernization of T-72 | Same |

The author predicts that **Omsktransmash will be the first to shut down** in 2026, followed by others through 2026–2027 as the remaining restoration potential is exhausted.

## 5. Conclusion: The End of Russian Tank Building

The war in Ukraine has **killed Russian tank manufacturing**—an industry already undermined by decades of monopoly-driven degradation and corruption.
- Russia entered the war with **no real new-build capacity** but with massive Soviet-era reserves.
- Those reserves have now been **depleted beyond recovery**.
- Within 2026–2027, the remaining restoration enterprises will run out of workable material.

The author concludes that Russian tank production will **never return to its February 23, 2022 level**, and the cumulative factors from the USSR’s collapse have led to the industry’s **inevitable demise**.

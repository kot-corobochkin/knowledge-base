## 1. Context: Official Information from Ukraine’s Commander-in-Chief

Information published on the official page of Commander-in-Chief General Oleksandr Syrskyi confirms a **systemic crisis** within Russian occupation forces—a trend that has been observed before but is now being underscored by official data.

**Key finding:** For the **third consecutive month**, Russia is mobilizing **fewer personnel** than it is losing in combat in Ukraine.

## 2. Russian Losses: December 2025 – February 2026

| Month | Total Losses | Estimated Killed (200s) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| December 2025 | 35,050 | ~23,000 |
| January 2026 | 31,710 | ~20,000 |
| February 2026 | 26,090 | ~17,000 |

- For over six months, **killed have consistently represented 60–65% of total losses**.
- February’s lower numbers are partly due to it being a **shortened month** (28 days). Adjusted to 31 days, losses would likely have exceeded **29,000**.

## 3. Russian Mobilization Capacity

Current mobilization in Russia is running at approximately **20,000–22,000 personnel per month**.

This means Russia is unable to fully compensate for:
- **Total monthly losses** (which average well above 22,000)
- In some months, not even **losses from killed alone** (which have ranged from 17,000 to 23,000)

## 4. The Personnel Deficit

| Comparison | Figure |
| --- | --- |
| Average monthly losses (last 3 months) | ~31,000 |
| Monthly mobilization | ~20,000–22,000 |
| Monthly shortfall | ~9,000–11,000 |

**Percentage shortfall:** between **25% and 37%** of required replacements.

## 6. Conclusion

The author notes that while some observers may focus on the **decrease in Russian losses** (from 35,050 in December to 26,090 in February) as a potentially negative trend, the **more significant systemic issue** is the **growing gap between losses and replacements**.

Even at the reduced loss levels, Russia is **not mobilizing enough** to sustain its current force structure. Without a change in mobilization policy, the occupation forces face an inevitable erosion of their offensive potential—a crisis that is systemic, not seasonal or temporary.

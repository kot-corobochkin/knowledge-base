[[Thought and Intelligence]]



## Summary

This chapter provides a foundational introduction to Artificial Intelligence (AI). It defines AI as the field dedicated to creating machines that can behave intelligently like humans. It traces the historical roots of AI, introduces the Turing Test as a seminal concept for defining machine intelligence, outlines the key characteristics of intelligent agents, and categorizes the four main approaches to AI research: acting humanly, thinking humanly, thinking rationally, and acting rationally.

## 1. Core Concept: What is Artificial Intelligence?
- **Motivation:** While computers excel at performing mechanical, rule-based tasks efficiently, they traditionally lack the human abilities of reasoning, common sense, and adaptation to new situations.
- **Goal of AI:** To create machines that behave like ordinary human beings, improving upon current machine capabilities to tackle complex tasks. AI aims to understand human intelligence by recreating its processes (abstract thought, reasoning, pattern recognition) in a machine.
## 2. Historical Roots
- **Ancient Foundations:** The concept of logical reasoning originated with the Greek philosopher Aristotle (2,400 years ago).
- **Key Developments:**

**George Boole:** Developed Boolean algebra in the 19th century, which became the foundation for computer circuits.
- **Alan Turing:** Proposed the Turing Test in 1950, a method to determine if a machine can think.
- **John McCarthy:** Coined the term "artificial intelligence" in 1956.
## 3. The Turing Test

The Turing Test is a seminal concept designed to answer the question, "Can machines think?"
- **Setup:**

There are three terminals: two operated by humans (a questioner and a respondent) and one by a computer.
- The questioner interrogates both the human and the computer respondents, receiving responses from both but not knowing which is which.
- The test is conducted within a specific subject area and time limit.

- **Passing the Test:** If the interrogator cannot reliably distinguish the computer's responses from the human's, the computer is said to have passed the test and is considered intelligent.
- **Required Capabilities for a Computer to Pass:**

**Natural Language Processing:** To communicate effectively.
- **Knowledge Representation:** To store information.
- **Automated Reasoning:** To use stored information to answer questions and draw conclusions.
- **Machine Learning:** To adapt to new circumstances.

- **The Total Turing Test:** An extended version that includes a video signal and the ability to pass objects. To pass this, a computer would also need **computer vision** and **robotics**.
- **Criticisms of the Turing Test:**

**Chinese Room Argument (John Searle):** A person following rules to manipulate Chinese 
symbols can create the illusion of understanding Chinese without actually comprehending it. Searle argued that a computer passing the Turing test is merely manipulating symbols without true understanding.
- **Limited Scope:** The test can be "gamed" by limiting questions to narrow fields (e.g., math) or simple "Yes/No" answers, which doesn't prove general intelligence.
- **Superhuman Performance:** A computer might perform so much better (e.g., in an information search) that it is easily identifiable, paradoxically failing the test.
## 6. Four Approaches to Artificial Intelligence

The field of AI can be categorized by its goal: to create systems that act/think like humans or that act/think rationally.

| Approach               | Goal                                                                      | Key Focus                               | Method / Challenge                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1. Acting Humanly      | To create systems that perform tasks as a human would.                    | Passing the Turing Test.                | Requires capabilities like natural language processing, knowledge representation, reasoning, and learning.                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| 2. Thinking Humanly    | To create systems whose internal reasoning processes model human thought. | Cognitive Modeling (Cognitive Science). | Uses introspection and psychological experiments to develop theories of the mind, which are then expressed as computer programs.                                                                                                                                                                    |
| 3. Thinking Rationally | To create systems that use formal, logical rules for reasoning.           | Laws of Thought (Formal Logic).         | Uses syllogisms and formal logic to derive correct conclusions from premises. Obstacles: Difficulty in representing informal or uncertain knowledge and the computational complexity of finding a solution.                                                                                         |
| 4. Acting Rationally   | To create systems that achieve their goals based on their beliefs.        | Rational Agents.                        | An agent perceives and acts to achieve goals. This is more general than the "thinking rationally" approach, as it includes actions based on reflexes or other non-logical mechanisms. It is considered more amenable to scientific development because the standard of rationality is well-defined. |
## 7. Summary of AI Definitions

The chapter concludes by listing various definitions of AI from different sources, all converging on the idea that AI is the study and creation of machines that perform functions requiring intelligence when done by humans. It encompasses subfields such as:
- **Game Playing** (e.g., Deep Blue vs. Kasparov)
- **Expert Systems** (e.g., medical diagnosis)
- **Natural Language Processing**
- **Neural Networks**
- **Robotics**

Common AI programming languages include **LISP** and **Prolog**.

## 1. Overview of the Attack

On the night of March 14, Russian occupation forces launched a **combined mass strike** against Ukraine, with **Kyiv** as the primary target. The attack employed nearly the entire range of missile systems currently available to the Russian military.

## 2. Missiles Used and Interception Rates

| Missile Type | Quantity Launched | Intercepted | Interception Rate |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 9M723 / KN-23 / 5V55 (ballistic) | 13 | 7 | 54% |
| 3M14 Kalibr (cruise missile) | 25 | 25 | 100% |
| Kh-101 (air-launched cruise missile) | 24 | 24 | 100% |
| 3M22 Tsirkon (hypersonic) | 2 | 1 | 50% |
| Kh-59/69 (air-to-surface) | 4 | 1 | 25% |
| Total | 67 | 58 | 86% |

## Key Observations
- **100% interception** of Kh-101 and Kalibr cruise missiles.
- Aviation assets played a significant role in these interceptions, contributing to record-breaking effectiveness against these targets.

## 3. Strategic Aviation Involvement
- **Aircraft used:** Tu-95MS and Tu-160 strategic bombers.
- **Number of aircraft:** Between **6 and 9**.
- **Payload:** Each aircraft carried no more than **4 missiles**, despite a theoretical capacity of up to 8. This suggests either:

A deliberate choice to avoid overloading aircraft, or
- **Limited availability of operational Tu-95MS** after Ukraine’s **"Web" (Pavutyna) operation**, which reduced the number of flight-capable units to a handful.

## 4. Drone Attack Component

| Type | Quantity Launched | Intercepted |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Shahed-136 kamikaze drones and decoys (total) | 430 | 402 |
| Of which: Shahed-136 strike drones | ~250 | (included in total) |

The drone component was substantial, with **over 400 drones intercepted**, reflecting sustained pressure from Russian unmanned systems alongside missiles.

## 5. Temporal Pattern and Strategic Context
- The previous combined mass strike occurred on **March 7**—exactly **one week earlier**.
- This confirms a systemic pattern of **approximately one combined act of terror per week**, as noted in the author’s earlier analysis.
- The absence of Kh-101 missiles in the previous strike, followed by their use in this one, suggests a **rotation of available munitions**. This may indicate not only stockpile management but also **limitations tied to available launch platforms** (particularly Tu-95MS bombers).

## 6. Conclusion: Predictability and Systemic Weaknesses

The author concludes that Russia’s systematic strikes are becoming **more predictable** and increasingly **reveal internal problems** faced by the occupation forces:
- Depletion of certain missile types
- Limited numbers of operational strategic bombers
- A fixed weekly tempo that reflects production and logistics constraints rather than strategic flexibility

Despite the scale of the attack, the high interception rates—particularly against cruise missiles—demonstrate the effectiveness of Ukrainian air defense, while the pattern of strikes exposes growing vulnerabilities in Russia’s ability to sustain such operations.

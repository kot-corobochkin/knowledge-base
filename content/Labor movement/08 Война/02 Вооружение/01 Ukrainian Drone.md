## 3. The Drone War: A Lesson Learned Too Slowly

## 3.1. The Pace of Adaptation

Russia constantly modifies its tactics and equipment, releasing **up to ten new modifications of attack drones per month**. As Kovalenko notes: "They do something new — we adapt. We do something new — they adapt. This happens every day."

Europe, meanwhile, often integrates Ukrainian solutions with a **delay of one to two years**, effectively adopting experience that is no longer relevant.

## 3.2. Ukraine’s Drone Capability Gap

Despite being the laboratory of drone warfare, Ukraine faces a **critical shortage of drone pilots**, according to Ivan Kyrychevskyi.
- Ukraine has been using drones since 2015–2016, initially for reconnaissance, now for both reconnaissance and strike missions.
- Until recently, pilot training was largely driven by volunteer initiatives. Formal drone units within the Armed Forces have only begun to emerge in the last **1.5 years**.
- The Unmanned Systems Forces as a full branch of the military are **still being formed**.

In contrast, Russia has moved faster and more ruthlessly in organizational terms, investing heavily not only in production but also in a structured training network and integration of drones into fire control systems.

## 3.3. No Myth of Uniqueness

Kyrychevskyi dispels the narrative that Ukraine is the world’s sole "teacher" in drone warfare. As early as 2015, Polish reconnaissance drones were technologically superior to Ukrainian ones. Ukraine learned, experimented, and absorbed experience. Today, the relationship is horizontal: solutions tested in Ukraine are trialed in Polish exercises, and vice versa.

Drones are **mass mobilization weapons**. Their strength lies not in the sacredness of technology but in **scale of production, operator training, and tactical integration**. In this sense, the relationship is one of evolution: "The student has surpassed the teacher, but the teacher in this case is Poland."
